Chapter 5: Elasticity

Discover how economists measure responsiveness. Elasticity helps explain how consumers and producers react to changes in prices, income, and related goods, making it one of the most important analytical tools in AP Microeconomics.

Chapter Overview

Elasticity measures the degree to which buyers and sellers respond to economic changes. In this chapter, students will learn how elasticity affects consumer behavior, business decisions, government taxation policies, and market outcomes.

Understanding elasticity allows economists to predict how changes in prices, income levels, and market conditions influence quantity demanded and quantity supplied. These concepts appear frequently on the AP Microeconomics Exam and are essential for analyzing real-world markets.

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price.

Some goods experience large changes in demand when prices change, while others experience only small changes. Price elasticity helps economists quantify this difference.


Elasticity is calculated by comparing the percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in price.


Understanding price elasticity is important for businesses because pricing decisions can significantly affect revenue and profitability.

Elastic Demand

Consumers respond strongly to price changes. A small price increase causes a large decrease in quantity demanded.

Inelastic Demand

Consumers respond weakly to price changes. Demand remains relatively stable despite changing prices.

Unit Elastic Demand

Percentage changes in quantity demanded and price are equal.

Determinants of Elasticity

Several factors influence how responsive consumers are to changes in price.

Availability of Substitutes

The more substitutes available, the more elastic demand tends to be.

Necessity vs Luxury

Necessities often have inelastic demand, while luxury goods are usually more elastic.

Percentage of Income

Goods that represent a large portion of income tend to be more elastic.

Time Period

Demand generally becomes more elastic over longer periods of time.

For example, gasoline tends to be relatively inelastic in the short run because people still need transportation. Over time, however, consumers may switch to fuel-efficient vehicles or alternative transportation, making demand more elastic.

Elastic vs Inelastic Demand

The value of elasticity determines how strongly quantity demanded responds to a change in price.

Elastic Demand

Demand is elastic when consumers respond strongly to price changes. A small increase in price leads to a relatively large decrease in quantity demanded.

  • Elasticity > 1
  • Luxury goods often exhibit elastic demand
  • Many substitutes available

Inelastic Demand

Demand is inelastic when quantity demanded changes only slightly after a price change.

  • Elasticity < 1
  • Necessities often exhibit inelastic demand
  • Few substitutes available

Unit Elastic Demand

A percentage change in price produces an equal percentage change in quantity demanded.

  • Elasticity = 1
  • Total revenue remains unchanged

Total Revenue Test

The Total Revenue Test helps determine whether demand is elastic or inelastic by examining how revenue changes when prices change.

Elastic Demand

Price and total revenue move in opposite directions.

  • Price ↑ → Revenue ↓
  • Price ↓ → Revenue ↑

Inelastic Demand

Price and total revenue move in the same direction.

  • Price ↑ → Revenue ↑
  • Price ↓ → Revenue ↓

Unit Elastic Demand

Changes in price do not affect total revenue.

Price Elasticity of Supply

Price Elasticity of Supply measures how responsive producers are to changes in market prices.

When prices increase, producers often increase output. The extent of this response determines the elasticity of supply.


Price Elasticity of Supply = Percentage Change in Quantity Supplied ÷ Percentage Change in Price


Supply tends to be more elastic when firms can easily adjust production and more inelastic when production requires significant time or resources.

Income Elasticity of Demand

Income Elasticity measures how demand changes when consumer income changes.

Normal Goods

Demand increases as income increases.

Examples: vacations, electronics, restaurant meals.

Inferior Goods

Demand decreases as income increases.

Examples may include generic products or public transportation.

Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

Cross-Price Elasticity measures how demand for one good changes when the price of another good changes.

Substitute Goods

When the price of one good rises, demand for the substitute increases.

Example: Coca-Cola and Pepsi.

Complementary Goods

When the price of one good rises, demand for the related product falls.

Example: printers and ink cartridges.

Applications of Elasticity

Elasticity is one of the most practical concepts in economics and is widely used by businesses and governments.

Business Pricing

Firms use elasticity to determine optimal pricing strategies.

Tax Policy

Governments analyze elasticity when deciding which goods to tax.

Revenue Forecasting

Elasticity helps predict how sales revenue may change after price adjustments.

Market Research

Companies estimate consumer responsiveness before launching products.

Real-World Elasticity Examples

Elasticity can be observed in many everyday markets.

Gasoline

In the short run, gasoline demand is often relatively inelastic because consumers still need transportation.


Luxury Cars

Luxury vehicles usually have elastic demand because consumers can delay purchases or choose alternatives.


Prescription Medicine

Essential medications often exhibit highly inelastic demand due to necessity.


Streaming Services

Subscription platforms face more elastic demand because consumers can easily switch providers.

Summary

Elasticity measures responsiveness in economic behavior and is a fundamental tool for understanding consumer choices, producer decisions, government policy, and market outcomes.

Price Elasticity of Demand measures how consumers respond to price changes. Demand may be elastic, inelastic, or unit elastic depending on consumer behavior.


Elasticity is influenced by factors such as the availability of substitutes, whether a good is a necessity or luxury, the share of income spent on the good, and the amount of time consumers have to adjust.


Additional elasticity measures—including Price Elasticity of Supply, Income Elasticity, and Cross-Price Elasticity—help economists analyze a wide variety of market situations and business decisions.

Key Terms

Review these important concepts before moving to the next chapter.

Elastic Demand

Demand with elasticity greater than 1.

Inelastic Demand

Demand with elasticity less than 1.

Unit Elastic

Elasticity exactly equal to 1.

Total Revenue

Price multiplied by quantity sold.

Price Elasticity of Supply

Responsiveness of quantity supplied to price changes.

Income Elasticity

Responsiveness of demand to changes in income.

Cross-Price Elasticity

Responsiveness of demand to price changes in another good.

Substitutes

Goods that can replace one another.

Complements

Goods that are commonly used together.

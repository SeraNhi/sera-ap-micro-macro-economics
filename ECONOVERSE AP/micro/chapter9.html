Chapter Overview

Perfect competition is rare in the real world. Many industries are dominated by firms that possess market power, allowing them to influence prices, output levels, and consumer choices. This chapter introduces imperfect competition and explores how monopolies, monopolistic competition, and oligopolies operate.

Understanding imperfect competition is essential because many modern industries—from technology companies to telecommunications providers— exhibit characteristics that differ significantly from perfect competition.

Learning Objectives

  • Define imperfect competition.
  • Understand the characteristics of monopoly.
  • Identify common barriers to entry.
  • Analyze how monopolies determine price and output.
  • Compare monopolies with competitive markets.
  • Evaluate the effects of market power on consumers.

Monopoly

A monopoly exists when a single seller controls the entire market for a product or service with no close substitutes available. Because there is only one producer, the monopolist possesses significant market power.

Unlike firms in perfect competition, monopolies are price makers rather than price takers. They can influence market prices by adjusting output.

AP Exam Tip: A monopoly is characterized by one seller, high barriers to entry, and substantial control over price.

Characteristics of Monopoly

Single Seller

One firm supplies the entire market.

No Close Substitutes

Consumers have few or no alternative products.

Price Maker

The firm can influence market prices through output decisions.

High Entry Barriers

New competitors face significant obstacles entering the market.

Barriers to Entry

Barriers to entry prevent or discourage new firms from entering an industry. These barriers help monopolies maintain market power over long periods.

Legal Barriers

Patents, copyrights, and government licenses can restrict competition.

Control of Resources

A firm may own a critical resource required for production.

Natural Monopoly

Large economies of scale allow one firm to produce more efficiently than multiple competitors.

High Startup Costs

Significant capital investment may discourage new entrants.

Profit Maximization for Monopoly

Like all firms, monopolies seek to maximize profit. However, unlike firms in perfectly competitive markets, monopolists can influence market prices.

A monopolist determines the profit-maximizing quantity by producing where marginal revenue (MR) equals marginal cost (MC). After selecting the quantity, the monopolist uses the demand curve to determine the highest price consumers are willing to pay.

AP Exam Tip: Monopoly profit maximization occurs where MR = MC, not where price equals marginal cost.

Marginal Revenue and Marginal Cost

Marginal revenue is the additional revenue generated by selling one more unit of output. Because monopolists face downward-sloping demand curves, marginal revenue is always less than price.

Marginal Revenue (MR)

Additional revenue from selling one more unit.

Marginal Cost (MC)

Additional cost incurred by producing one more unit.

Profit Rule

Firms maximize profit where MR = MC.

Deadweight Loss

Monopolies often produce less output and charge higher prices than competitive markets. As a result, some mutually beneficial transactions never occur.

This loss of economic efficiency is called deadweight loss. Deadweight loss represents the reduction in total surplus caused by market power.

Key Concept: Deadweight loss is one of the primary reasons governments regulate monopolies.

Price Discrimination

Price discrimination occurs when a firm charges different prices to different consumers for the same product or service.

Successful price discrimination requires market power, the ability to separate consumers into groups, and limited resale opportunities.

Student Discounts

Lower prices offered to students.

Airline Tickets

Different travelers often pay different prices.

Movie Theaters

Senior and child discounts are common examples.

Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition combines elements of competition and monopoly. Many firms sell differentiated products that are similar but not identical.

Firms compete through branding, advertising, quality, and product features.

Many Sellers

Numerous firms operate within the market.

Product Differentiation

Products are unique but serve similar purposes.

Easy Entry

New firms can enter relatively easily.

Oligopoly

An oligopoly exists when a small number of large firms dominate a market. Because there are few competitors, each firm's decisions significantly affect the others.

Oligopolies often feature strategic behavior, advertising competition, and barriers to entry.

Few Firms

A small number of firms control most market share.

Interdependence

Firms must consider competitors' actions.

Barriers to Entry

High startup costs often limit new competition.

Game Theory

Game theory studies how firms make decisions when outcomes depend on the actions of other firms.

One of the most famous game theory models is the Prisoner's Dilemma, which illustrates why firms may fail to cooperate even when cooperation would benefit all participants.

AP Exam Tip: Game theory is commonly used to explain pricing and strategic behavior in oligopolistic markets.

Real-World Examples

  • Public utilities often resemble natural monopolies.
  • Smartphone operating systems display characteristics of oligopoly.
  • Fast-food restaurants commonly operate under monopolistic competition.
  • Airlines frequently engage in price discrimination.
  • Streaming services compete strategically within oligopolistic markets.

AP Economics Connections

Market structures are heavily tested on the AP Microeconomics exam. Students should be comfortable comparing monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and perfect competition using graphs, profit analysis, and efficiency measures.

Pay special attention to profit maximization, deadweight loss, price discrimination, and strategic behavior because these topics frequently appear in both MCQs and FRQs.

Summary

Imperfect competition occurs when firms possess market power and can influence prices. This chapter explored monopolies, monopolistic competition, and oligopolies, along with the strategic behavior that often characterizes these market structures.

Monopolies maximize profit where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, may engage in price discrimination, and often create deadweight loss. Monopolistic competition combines many sellers with product differentiation, while oligopolies feature interdependent firms whose decisions influence one another.

Key Terms

Monopoly

A market with a single seller.

Barrier to Entry

Obstacle preventing new firms from entering a market.

Marginal Revenue

Additional revenue from selling one more unit.

Deadweight Loss

Loss of total surplus caused by inefficiency.

Price Discrimination

Charging different prices to different consumers.

Monopolistic Competition

Many firms selling differentiated products.

Oligopoly

A market dominated by a few large firms.

Game Theory

The study of strategic decision-making.

Multiple-Choice Review Questions

  1. Which market structure contains a single seller with no close substitutes?

    A. Perfect Competition
    B. Oligopoly
    C. Monopoly
    D. Monopolistic Competition

  2. A monopolist maximizes profit when:

    A. Price = Marginal Cost
    B. Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost
    C. Total Revenue = Total Cost
    D. Demand = Supply

  3. Deadweight loss occurs because monopolies:

    A. Produce too much output
    B. Produce less output than efficient levels
    C. Eliminate all profits
    D. Increase consumer surplus

  4. Which market structure is most associated with strategic behavior?

    A. Perfect Competition
    B. Monopoly
    C. Oligopoly
    D. Pure Competition

Answers: 1-C, 2-B, 3-B, 4-C

Chapter Summary

  • Monopolies are single-seller markets with significant market power.
  • Profit maximization occurs where MR = MC.
  • Monopolies often generate deadweight loss.
  • Price discrimination allows firms to charge different prices to different consumers.
  • Monopolistic competition involves differentiated products and many firms.
  • Oligopolies are characterized by interdependence and strategic behavior.
  • Game theory helps explain decision-making in oligopolistic markets.
  • Market structures are heavily tested on the AP Microeconomics Exam.

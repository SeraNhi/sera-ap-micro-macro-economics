Chapter 10: Resource Markets

Explore how firms hire resources, determine wages, and make employment decisions using derived demand and marginal revenue product analysis.

Chapter Overview

Resource markets focus on the buying and selling of factors of production such as labor, land, capital, and entrepreneurship. This chapter explains how firms determine the quantity of resources they employ and how factor prices are established.

Unlike product markets where consumers demand goods and services, resource markets involve firms demanding inputs needed for production. Understanding these markets helps explain wages, employment levels, and business hiring decisions.

Learning Objectives

Understand Derived Demand

Explain why the demand for resources depends on the demand for the products those resources help produce.

Calculate MRP

Understand how marginal revenue product measures the value of an additional worker or resource.

Analyze Hiring Decisions

Examine how firms determine the profit-maximizing quantity of labor and other inputs.

Derived Demand

The demand for a resource is derived from the demand for the final product that the resource helps produce.

Consumers do not directly purchase labor from firms. Instead, firms hire labor because workers help produce goods and services that consumers want to buy. Therefore, the demand for labor depends on the demand for the output being produced.


If consumer demand for smartphones increases, firms may hire additional workers to expand production. If demand falls, firms may reduce employment. This relationship is known as derived demand.


Key factors affecting derived demand include:

  • Demand for the final product
  • Productivity of the resource
  • Price of the final product
  • Availability of substitute resources

Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)

Marginal Revenue Product measures the additional revenue generated by employing one more unit of a resource.

MRP is one of the most important concepts in resource market analysis because firms use it when deciding how many workers to hire.


The basic formula is:

MRP = Marginal Product × Marginal Revenue


A profit-maximizing firm continues hiring additional workers as long as the revenue generated by the worker exceeds or equals the cost of employing that worker.


As additional workers are hired, diminishing marginal returns often cause MRP to decline, creating a downward-sloping resource demand curve.

Labor Markets

Labor markets bring together workers who supply labor and firms that demand labor. Wages and employment levels are determined through the interaction of supply and demand.

The labor market operates similarly to other markets. Workers supply labor in exchange for wages, while firms demand labor because workers contribute to production and revenue generation.


The labor demand curve is derived from Marginal Revenue Product (MRP), while the labor supply curve reflects workers' willingness to work at different wage rates.

Wage Determination

Wages are determined by the interaction of labor demand and labor supply.

Higher Demand

When demand for workers increases, wages and employment tend to rise.

Higher Supply

When more workers enter a labor market, wages may decrease if demand remains unchanged.

Skill Levels

Workers with specialized skills often earn higher wages due to greater productivity.

Perfectly Competitive Labor Markets

In a perfectly competitive labor market, many firms compete for workers and no single firm can influence wages.

Firms are wage takers and must accept the market wage rate. Because each additional worker costs the same wage, the firm's labor supply curve is perfectly elastic.


Profit-maximizing firms hire workers until:

MRP = Wage Rate

Monopsony

A monopsony exists when there is only one major buyer of labor in a market.

Unlike a competitive labor market, a monopsonist has significant control over wages because workers have limited employment alternatives.


Examples may include a large employer in a small town or a dominant healthcare provider hiring nurses in a rural region.


Monopsonies generally hire fewer workers and pay lower wages than competitive labor markets.

Labor Unions

Labor unions are organizations formed by workers to improve wages, benefits, and working conditions.

Through collective bargaining, unions negotiate contracts with employers on behalf of workers.


Supporters argue that unions increase worker bargaining power, while critics argue that unions may create labor market inefficiencies.


AP Economics often examines how unions affect wages, employment, and labor market outcomes.

Human Capital

Human capital refers to the knowledge, education, skills, training, and experience possessed by workers.

Investments in education and training typically increase worker productivity and earning potential.


Examples include earning a university degree, obtaining certifications, participating in internships, and developing specialized technical skills.


Higher levels of human capital generally lead to higher wages because employers value more productive workers.

Income Inequality

Income inequality refers to differences in earnings among individuals and groups within an economy.

Economists identify several causes of income inequality, including differences in education, skill levels, experience, technology, discrimination, and access to opportunities.


Governments may attempt to reduce income inequality through progressive taxation, social programs, education investments, and labor market policies.

Real-World Labor Market Examples

Technology Industry

Software engineers often earn high wages due to strong demand and specialized human capital.

Healthcare Sector

Doctors and nurses receive extensive training, increasing their productivity and earning potential.

Professional Sports

Elite athletes earn high incomes because their talents are scarce and generate substantial revenue.

AP Economics Connections

For the AP Exam, students should understand how labor markets operate, how wages are determined, how MRP influences hiring decisions, and how labor market structures affect employment outcomes.


Be prepared to analyze labor market graphs, calculate MRP, evaluate monopsony outcomes, and explain the effects of labor unions and human capital investments.

Summary

Resource markets determine the prices and quantities of productive inputs. Firms demand labor because workers contribute to production and revenue generation. The concepts of derived demand and marginal revenue product help explain hiring decisions and wage determination.


Students should understand competitive labor markets, monopsonies, labor unions, human capital, and income inequality, all of which are important AP Microeconomics topics.

Chapter 7: Costs of Production

Discover how economists analyze business costs, profits, and production decisions. This chapter explores explicit and implicit costs, economic profit, accounting profit, and the foundations of cost analysis used throughout AP Microeconomics.

Chapter Overview

Firms constantly make decisions about production, pricing, hiring, and investment. To make effective choices, businesses must understand the costs associated with producing goods and services. Economists examine these costs differently from accountants, leading to important distinctions between accounting profit and economic profit.

This chapter introduces the key cost concepts that underpin producer behavior. Students will learn how businesses evaluate resources, calculate profits, and determine whether production decisions create value.


The study of production costs also prepares students for later topics such as cost curves, profit maximization, market structures, and long-run business growth.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

Identify Costs

Distinguish between explicit and implicit costs of production.

Analyze Profit

Differentiate economic profit from accounting profit.

Apply Opportunity Cost

Recognize how opportunity costs influence business decisions.

Evaluate Firms

Understand how firms determine whether production is worthwhile.

Explicit Costs

Explicit costs are direct monetary payments made by firms for resources used in production.

Explicit costs involve actual cash expenditures. These are the costs that appear on accounting records and financial statements because money physically changes hands.


Examples include:

  • Employee wages and salaries
  • Rent payments
  • Utility bills
  • Raw materials
  • Equipment purchases
  • Advertising expenses
  • Insurance premiums

AP Economics Tip: Explicit costs are easy to identify because they involve observable financial transactions.

Implicit Costs

Implicit costs represent the opportunity costs of using resources already owned by the firm.

Unlike explicit costs, implicit costs do not require direct cash payments. Instead, they represent the value of opportunities sacrificed when resources are used in a particular way.


For example, if an entrepreneur leaves a job paying $60,000 per year to start a business, the forgone salary becomes an implicit cost of operating that business.


Other examples include:

  • Forgone wages
  • Forgone rental income from owned property
  • Interest that could have been earned elsewhere
  • Alternative uses of business-owned resources

Economists include implicit costs because they reflect the true opportunity cost of production decisions.

Economic Profit vs Accounting Profit

Economists and accountants measure profit differently because they treat costs differently.

Accounting profit considers only explicit costs:


Accounting Profit = Total Revenue − Explicit Costs


Economic profit considers both explicit and implicit costs:


Economic Profit = Total Revenue − (Explicit Costs + Implicit Costs)


Because economic profit includes opportunity costs, it usually provides a more complete picture of business performance.


A firm may earn a positive accounting profit while earning zero or even negative economic profit if implicit costs are substantial.

Fixed Costs

Fixed costs remain constant regardless of the quantity of output produced.

A fixed cost is a cost that does not change as production levels change. Whether a firm produces zero units or thousands of units, these costs must still be paid.


Common examples include:

  • Building rent
  • Property taxes
  • Business licenses
  • Insurance premiums
  • Long-term equipment leases

Because fixed costs do not vary with output, they become less significant per unit as production increases.

AP Economics Tip: Fixed costs exist even when output equals zero.

Variable Costs

Variable costs change as the level of production changes.

Variable costs rise when firms produce more output and fall when production decreases. These costs are directly tied to the production process.


Examples include:

  • Raw materials
  • Hourly labor
  • Packaging materials
  • Shipping expenses
  • Electricity used in production

As output expands, firms generally require additional resources, causing variable costs to increase.

Remember: No production usually means no variable costs.

Total Cost

Total cost combines all fixed and variable costs incurred by a firm.

Economists calculate total cost using a simple formula:


TC = FC + VC

Where:

  • TC = Total Cost
  • FC = Fixed Cost
  • VC = Variable Cost

For example, if a bakery pays $2,000 in monthly rent and spends $3,000 on ingredients and labor, total cost equals $5,000.

Average Costs

Average costs measure production costs on a per-unit basis.

Average costs help firms determine how much each unit of output costs to produce.


Key average cost measures include:

  • Average Fixed Cost (AFC)
  • Average Variable Cost (AVC)
  • Average Total Cost (ATC)

Average Fixed Cost decreases as production increases because fixed costs are spread across more units of output.


Average Total Cost combines both fixed and variable costs per unit produced.

Marginal Cost

Marginal cost measures the additional cost of producing one more unit of output.

Marginal cost is one of the most important concepts in microeconomics because firms use it when deciding how much output to produce.


MC=\frac{\Delta TC}{\Delta Q}

Marginal cost focuses on changes rather than totals. It tells producers how costs change when output increases by one unit.


Profit-maximizing firms compare marginal cost with marginal revenue when making production decisions.

AP Exam Connection: Marginal Cost appears throughout AP Microeconomics and is critical for understanding firm behavior.

Cost Curves

Cost curves visually represent the relationship between costs and output.

Economists use several cost curves to analyze production efficiency:

  • Average Fixed Cost (AFC)
  • Average Variable Cost (AVC)
  • Average Total Cost (ATC)
  • Marginal Cost (MC)

One important AP Economics relationship is that the Marginal Cost curve intersects both the AVC and ATC curves at their minimum points.


Understanding these graphs is essential for later chapters involving perfect competition, monopoly, and profit maximization.

Economies of Scale

Large firms often benefit from lower costs as production expands.

Economies of scale occur when average costs decrease as output increases.


Sources of economies of scale include:

  • Specialization of labor
  • Bulk purchasing discounts
  • Advanced technology
  • Managerial efficiency

Large companies such as major retailers and manufacturers frequently achieve cost advantages because of economies of scale.

Diseconomies of Scale

Growth does not always reduce costs.

Diseconomies of scale occur when average costs increase as firms become too large.


Common causes include:

  • Communication problems
  • Management inefficiencies
  • Coordination difficulties
  • Bureaucratic delays

When firms become excessively large, managing operations can become more complex, causing costs to rise.

Real-World Applications

Understanding production costs helps explain business decisions across industries.

Airlines experience extremely high fixed costs due to aircraft purchases, airport facilities, and maintenance infrastructure.


Restaurants face significant variable costs because food ingredients and labor expenses change with customer demand.


Technology companies often benefit from economies of scale because software can be distributed to millions of users at relatively low additional cost.


Economic cost analysis helps businesses determine prices, production levels, expansion plans, and investment decisions.

Summary

This chapter introduced the major cost concepts used by economists to analyze firm behavior and production decisions.

Firms face both explicit costs and implicit costs when producing goods and services. While accountants focus on direct monetary expenditures, economists also consider opportunity costs when evaluating business performance.


Students learned how fixed costs, variable costs, total costs, average costs, and marginal costs influence production decisions. Understanding these concepts provides the foundation for analyzing market structures and profit-maximizing behavior.


The chapter also explored economies of scale and diseconomies of scale, demonstrating how firm size can affect production efficiency.

Key Terms

Review these essential AP Microeconomics terms.

Explicit Cost

Direct monetary payment for resources.

Implicit Cost

Opportunity cost of using owned resources.

Accounting Profit

Total revenue minus explicit costs.

Economic Profit

Total revenue minus all explicit and implicit costs.

Fixed Cost

Cost that does not change with output.

Variable Cost

Cost that changes as output changes.

Total Cost

Fixed cost plus variable cost.

Marginal Cost

Additional cost of producing one more unit.

Average Total Cost

Total cost per unit of output.

Economies of Scale

Declining average costs as output increases.

Diseconomies of Scale

Increasing average costs as output increases.

Chapter Summary

  • Explicit costs involve direct monetary payments.
  • Implicit costs represent opportunity costs.
  • Accounting profit excludes implicit costs.
  • Economic profit includes all opportunity costs.
  • Fixed costs do not change with output.
  • Variable costs rise as production increases.
  • Total cost equals fixed cost plus variable cost.
  • Marginal cost measures the cost of one additional unit.
  • Economies of scale reduce average costs.
  • Diseconomies of scale increase average costs.

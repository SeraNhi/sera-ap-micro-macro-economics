Chapter 13: Inflation and Unemployment

Understand how economists measure changes in prices, analyze inflation trends, evaluate labor market performance, and interpret key macroeconomic indicators used throughout AP Macroeconomics.

Chapter Overview

Inflation and unemployment are two of the most important indicators of economic performance. Governments, businesses, and households closely monitor both because they influence purchasing power, economic growth, living standards, and public policy decisions.

In this chapter, you will learn how inflation is measured through the Consumer Price Index (CPI), how inflation rates are calculated, and why changes in the overall price level affect economic decision-making. You will also develop the foundation needed to understand unemployment and labor market performance in later sections.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Define inflation and explain why it occurs.
  • Understand how economists construct the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
  • Calculate inflation rates using CPI data.
  • Interpret changes in purchasing power over time.
  • Analyze the effects of inflation on consumers and businesses.
  • Connect inflation data to AP Macroeconomics exam questions.

Inflation

Inflation refers to a sustained increase in the overall price level of goods and services within an economy over time.

When inflation occurs, money loses purchasing power because consumers must pay higher prices for the same goods and services. Moderate inflation is common in growing economies, but excessive inflation can create uncertainty and reduce economic stability.


For example, if a textbook costs $100 today and $105 next year, the increase in price contributes to inflation. Economists focus on changes in the average price level across many products rather than the price of a single good.


Inflation affects wages, savings, investments, interest rates, and government policy decisions. Because of its importance, economists use several indicators to measure inflation accurately.

Consumer Price Index (CPI)

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is one of the most widely used measures of inflation.

The CPI measures the average change in prices paid by consumers for a fixed basket of goods and services over time. This basket includes items commonly purchased by households such as food, housing, transportation, clothing, healthcare, and entertainment.


Economists compare the cost of the basket in different years to determine how much prices have changed.

CPI Formula

CPI = (Cost of Market Basket in Current Year ÷ Cost of Market Basket in Base Year) × 100

The base year is assigned a value of 100. CPI values above 100 indicate that prices have risen relative to the base year, while values below 100 indicate lower prices than the base year.

Calculating Inflation Rate

Economists use CPI data to calculate the percentage change in prices from one year to another.

The inflation rate measures how rapidly the general price level changes over time.

Inflation Rate Formula

Inflation Rate (%) = [(Current CPI − Previous CPI) ÷ Previous CPI] × 100

Example:

  • Previous Year CPI = 120
  • Current Year CPI = 126

Inflation Rate = [(126 − 120) ÷ 120] × 100 = 5%


This means that the average price level increased by 5 percent during the year.

Understanding inflation calculations is essential for AP Macroeconomics because students are frequently asked to calculate and interpret inflation rates using economic data tables and graphs.

Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand-pull inflation occurs when total spending in the economy grows faster than the economy's ability to produce goods and services.

Economists often summarize demand-pull inflation with the phrase: "Too much money chasing too few goods."


As households, businesses, and governments increase spending, producers may struggle to keep up with rising demand. As a result, prices increase.


Common causes of demand-pull inflation include:

  • Increased consumer spending
  • Expansionary fiscal policy
  • Expansionary monetary policy
  • Strong business investment
  • Growing export demand

Demand-pull inflation is frequently illustrated using shifts in Aggregate Demand (AD) within AP Macroeconomics.

Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation occurs when production costs increase, causing businesses to raise prices.

Unlike demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation originates on the supply side of the economy.


Businesses may face higher costs due to:

  • Rising wages
  • Higher energy prices
  • Increased raw material costs
  • Supply chain disruptions
  • Natural disasters affecting production

For example, a sharp increase in oil prices raises transportation and manufacturing costs. Firms often pass these costs on to consumers through higher prices.


In AP Macroeconomics, cost-push inflation is commonly represented by a leftward shift of Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS).

Consequences of Inflation

Inflation affects different groups within the economy in different ways.

Reduced Purchasing Power

Consumers can purchase fewer goods and services with the same amount of money.

Uncertainty

Businesses may find it more difficult to plan future investments and pricing strategies.

Savers Lose

Money held in savings accounts may lose value if inflation exceeds interest rates.

Borrowers Benefit

Loans may become easier to repay because future dollars are worth less.

Menu Costs

Firms incur costs when frequently updating prices and catalogs.

Shoe Leather Costs

People spend more time managing cash and financial decisions during inflationary periods.

Unemployment

Unemployment measures the percentage of people in the labor force who are actively seeking work but unable to find employment.

The labor force includes individuals who are either employed or actively searching for employment.


People who are retired, full-time students not seeking work, or those who have stopped looking for jobs are generally not counted as unemployed.

Unemployment Rate Formula

(Number of Unemployed ÷ Labor Force) × 100

Monitoring unemployment helps economists evaluate economic health and labor market conditions.

Types of Unemployment

Economists classify unemployment into several categories based on its causes.

Frictional Unemployment

Temporary unemployment occurring when workers move between jobs or enter the workforce.

Structural Unemployment

Occurs when workers' skills no longer match available jobs because of technological change or shifts in economic activity.

Cyclical Unemployment

Results from economic downturns and recessions when overall demand for labor declines.

Seasonal Unemployment

Occurs because demand for labor varies throughout the year in industries such as tourism and agriculture.

Natural Rate of Unemployment

Even healthy economies experience some unemployment.

The natural rate of unemployment consists primarily of frictional and structural unemployment.


Because workers continuously change jobs and industries evolve over time, some level of unemployment is unavoidable.


The natural rate does not include cyclical unemployment caused by recessions.


Economists use the natural rate as a benchmark when evaluating labor market performance.

Full Employment

Full employment does not mean zero unemployment.

Full employment occurs when cyclical unemployment is absent and the economy is operating at its potential output.


At full employment, only frictional and structural unemployment remain.


Most economists consider an economy to be at full employment when unemployment is approximately equal to the natural rate of unemployment.

Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve illustrates a historical relationship between inflation and unemployment.

In the short run, lower unemployment is often associated with higher inflation, while higher unemployment is often associated with lower inflation.


This relationship suggests a short-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment.


Policymakers frequently analyze the Phillips Curve when considering monetary and fiscal policy decisions.


Although the relationship does not always hold perfectly, the Phillips Curve remains an important AP Macroeconomics concept.

Real-World Examples

Economic indicators become more meaningful when applied to actual events.

During periods of strong economic growth, increased consumer spending can contribute to demand-pull inflation as businesses struggle to meet rising demand.


Sharp increases in oil prices have historically contributed to cost-push inflation by raising production and transportation costs throughout the economy.


Economic recessions often increase cyclical unemployment as firms reduce production and lay off workers.


Technological advancements can create structural unemployment when workers must develop new skills to remain competitive in changing industries.

Summary

This chapter introduced two of the most important indicators of macroeconomic performance: inflation and unemployment.

Inflation measures changes in the overall price level and is commonly tracked using the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Economists calculate inflation rates to determine how rapidly prices change over time and to evaluate purchasing power.


Inflation can result from increasing aggregate demand (demand-pull inflation) or rising production costs (cost-push inflation). Both forms affect consumers, businesses, governments, and financial markets.


Unemployment measures the percentage of people actively seeking work who cannot find employment. Economists classify unemployment into frictional, structural, cyclical, and seasonal categories.


The natural rate of unemployment and the Phillips Curve help economists analyze labor market conditions and inflation-unemployment relationships.

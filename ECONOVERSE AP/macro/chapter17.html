Chapter 17: Monetary Policy

Discover how central banks influence economic activity through monetary policy. Learn how changes in interest rates, money supply, and Federal Reserve actions affect inflation, unemployment, and economic growth.

Chapter Overview

Monetary policy is one of the most powerful tools available to policymakers. By influencing interest rates and the money supply, central banks can stabilize the economy, manage inflation, and promote employment.

In this chapter, you will explore how the Federal Reserve conducts monetary policy, examine expansionary and contractionary strategies, and analyze how monetary policy affects aggregate demand and overall economic performance.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

Define Monetary Policy

Explain how central banks use monetary policy to influence economic activity.

Analyze Policy Tools

Understand the major tools used by the Federal Reserve.

Evaluate Economic Effects

Examine how monetary policy impacts inflation, unemployment, and growth.

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy refers to actions taken by a central bank to influence the money supply, interest rates, and overall economic conditions.

In the United States, monetary policy is conducted by the Federal Reserve System. The Federal Reserve uses various tools to achieve its dual mandate of promoting price stability and maximum employment.


Changes in monetary policy can influence borrowing, spending, investment, inflation, and economic growth throughout the economy.

AP Economics Focus: Monetary policy primarily affects Aggregate Demand by changing interest rates and the availability of credit.

Expansionary Monetary Policy

Expansionary monetary policy is used during recessions or periods of slow economic growth.

Lower Interest Rates

Encourages households and businesses to borrow and spend more.

Increase Money Supply

Provides greater liquidity throughout the financial system.

Boost Aggregate Demand

Helps increase output, employment, and economic growth.

Expansionary monetary policy shifts Aggregate Demand to the right, increasing real GDP and reducing cyclical unemployment.

Contractionary Monetary Policy

Contractionary monetary policy is used when inflation becomes excessively high.

Raise Interest Rates

Discourages borrowing and spending throughout the economy.

Reduce Money Supply

Limits liquidity and slows economic activity.

Control Inflation

Helps reduce upward pressure on prices.

Contractionary monetary policy shifts Aggregate Demand to the left, reducing inflationary pressures while potentially slowing economic growth in the short run.


Policymakers must carefully balance inflation control with maintaining strong employment and sustainable economic growth.

Open Market Operations

Open Market Operations (OMOs) are the buying and selling of government securities by the Federal Reserve to influence the money supply.

Buying Securities

When the Federal Reserve buys government bonds, money enters the banking system and the money supply increases.

Selling Securities

When the Federal Reserve sells government bonds, money leaves the banking system and the money supply decreases.

Most Frequently Used Tool

Open market operations are the primary tool used by the Federal Reserve to conduct monetary policy.

Open market operations directly affect bank reserves, making them a highly effective method for influencing lending, spending, and economic activity.

Discount Rate

The discount rate is the interest rate charged by the Federal Reserve when lending funds directly to commercial banks.

Lower Discount Rate

Encourages banks to borrow more reserves and expand lending.

Higher Discount Rate

Discourages borrowing from the Federal Reserve and reduces lending activity.

Monetary Policy Impact

Changes in the discount rate can influence the overall money supply.

Reserve Requirements

Reserve requirements determine the percentage of deposits banks must keep as reserves rather than lend out.

A lower reserve requirement allows banks to make more loans, increasing the money supply. A higher reserve requirement limits lending and decreases the potential expansion of the money supply.


Although reserve requirements are a powerful monetary policy tool, they are adjusted less frequently because changes can significantly affect banking operations.

Federal Funds Rate

The federal funds rate is the interest rate banks charge one another for overnight loans of reserves.

Key Benchmark Rate

Many other interest rates in the economy are influenced by the federal funds rate.

Economic Influence

Changes in the federal funds rate affect borrowing, spending, and investment decisions.

Policy Target

The Federal Reserve often targets a specific federal funds rate range through open market operations.

Money Supply and Interest Rates

Monetary policy influences economic activity primarily through changes in the money supply and interest rates.

When the money supply increases, interest rates generally fall. Lower interest rates encourage borrowing, investment, and consumer spending.


When the money supply decreases, interest rates generally rise. Higher interest rates discourage borrowing and reduce spending throughout the economy.


These changes directly affect Aggregate Demand and overall economic performance.

Monetary Policy Transmission Mechanism

The transmission mechanism describes how monetary policy actions affect the broader economy.

Federal Reserve Action → Changes in Bank Reserves → Changes in Interest Rates → Changes in Borrowing and Spending → Changes in Aggregate Demand → Changes in GDP, Employment, and Inflation


Because individuals and businesses need time to adjust their behavior, monetary policy often affects the economy with significant time lags.

Real-World Monetary Policy Examples

Central banks around the world regularly use monetary policy to address economic challenges.

Economic Recessions

Central banks often lower interest rates and increase money supply to stimulate economic growth.

High Inflation

Central banks may raise interest rates to reduce spending and control inflation.

Financial Crises

Emergency lending programs and liquidity support can help stabilize financial systems.

Recent global economic events have demonstrated the important role central banks play in maintaining financial stability and supporting economic recovery.

Summary

Monetary policy is one of the most important tools used by central banks to stabilize the economy and promote sustainable growth.

Throughout this chapter, you learned how the Federal Reserve influences economic activity through changes in the money supply and interest rates. Expansionary monetary policy is used to stimulate growth during recessions, while contractionary monetary policy helps control inflation.


You also explored the major tools of monetary policy, including open market operations, the discount rate, reserve requirements, and the federal funds rate. Understanding these tools is essential for success in AP Macroeconomics.

Key Terms

Important vocabulary from Chapter 17.

Monetary Policy

Central bank actions designed to influence money supply and interest rates.

Expansionary Monetary Policy

Policy actions that increase money supply and lower interest rates.

Contractionary Monetary Policy

Policy actions that decrease money supply and raise interest rates.

Open Market Operations

The buying and selling of government securities by the Federal Reserve.

Discount Rate

The interest rate charged on loans from the Federal Reserve to banks.

Reserve Requirement

The percentage of deposits banks must hold as reserves.

Federal Funds Rate

The interest rate banks charge one another for overnight reserve loans.

Money Supply

The total quantity of money available in the economy.

Interest Rate

The cost of borrowing money.

Federal Reserve

The central banking system of the United States.

Chapter Summary

Key takeaways from Chapter 17.

  • Monetary policy is conducted by central banks to influence economic activity.
  • Expansionary monetary policy increases money supply and lowers interest rates.
  • Contractionary monetary policy decreases money supply and raises interest rates.
  • Open market operations are the Federal Reserve's primary policy tool.
  • The discount rate affects bank borrowing from the Federal Reserve.
  • Reserve requirements influence banks' lending capacity.
  • The federal funds rate is a key benchmark interest rate.
  • Interest rates affect consumption, investment, and Aggregate Demand.
  • Monetary policy affects the economy through a transmission mechanism.
  • The Federal Reserve plays a critical role in maintaining economic stability.

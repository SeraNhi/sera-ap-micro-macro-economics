Chapter 16: Money and Banking

Explore how money functions within modern economies, how banks create money through lending, and how financial institutions influence economic activity. Understanding money and banking provides the foundation for monetary policy and economic stability.

Chapter Overview

Money and banking are central to the functioning of every modern economy. Individuals, businesses, and governments rely on financial institutions to facilitate transactions, provide loans, and support economic growth.

In this chapter, you will examine the functions and characteristics of money, learn how money is measured, and understand how banks contribute to the expansion of the money supply. These concepts form the foundation for later chapters on monetary policy and central banking.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

Define Money

Explain what money is and why societies use it as a medium of exchange.

Identify Money Functions

Describe the major roles money plays in economic transactions.

Understand Banking

Analyze how banks influence the money supply and economic activity.

Functions of Money

Money performs several essential functions that make modern economies possible.

Medium of Exchange

Money eliminates the need for barter and allows goods and services to be exchanged efficiently.

Unit of Account

Money provides a common measure of value that makes prices easy to compare.

Store of Value

Money allows individuals to save purchasing power for future use.

Standard of Deferred Payment

Money enables borrowing and lending by providing a widely accepted payment standard.

Without money, individuals would need to find trading partners who possess exactly what they want and simultaneously desire what they have to offer. Money solves this problem and significantly improves economic efficiency.

Characteristics of Money

For an item to function effectively as money, it must possess several important characteristics.

Durability

Money must withstand repeated use without significant deterioration.

Portability

Money should be easy to transport and exchange.

Divisibility

Money must be capable of being divided into smaller units for transactions of different sizes.

Uniformity

Each unit of money should be identical in value to other units.

Limited Supply

Money must be relatively scarce to maintain value.

Acceptability

People must trust and accept money as payment for goods and services.

Money Supply

The money supply represents the total quantity of money available within an economy at a given time.

Economists and policymakers monitor the money supply because it influences spending, investment, inflation, and economic growth. Changes in the money supply can affect interest rates and overall economic activity.


Central banks closely track money supply measures to help achieve macroeconomic objectives such as stable prices, full employment, and sustainable economic growth.


In the next section, you will learn how economists classify different forms of money and how banks contribute to money creation through the lending process.

M1 and M2

Economists use different measures of the money supply to track the amount of money circulating throughout the economy.

M1

M1 includes the most liquid forms of money, such as currency in circulation, checking account deposits, and traveler's checks.

M2

M2 includes all components of M1 plus savings deposits, money market accounts, and small time deposits.

M1 is considered money that can be spent immediately, while M2 includes assets that can quickly be converted into spendable funds.

Fractional Reserve Banking

Modern banking systems operate under fractional reserve banking, where banks keep only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the remainder.

When customers deposit money into a bank, the bank is not required to keep the entire deposit in its vault. Instead, a portion is held as reserves while the remainder can be loaned to borrowers.


This process allows banks to create additional money within the economy and support economic growth through lending activities.

Required Reserves

Required reserves are the minimum amount of deposits that banks must keep and cannot lend out.

Purpose

Required reserves help ensure banks have sufficient funds available to meet withdrawal requests.

Financial Stability

Reserve requirements contribute to confidence in the banking system.

Monetary Policy Tool

Central banks may adjust reserve requirements to influence lending activity.

Excess Reserves

Excess reserves are reserves held by banks beyond the required minimum.

Banks may choose to hold excess reserves when economic uncertainty is high or when they expect lower demand for loans.


Large amounts of excess reserves generally reduce the money creation process because fewer funds are being loaned to borrowers.

Money Creation Process

Banks create money through the lending process within the fractional reserve banking system.

Suppose a customer deposits $1,000 into a bank with a 10% reserve requirement. The bank keeps $100 as reserves and loans out $900.


When the borrower spends the $900, it is deposited into another bank. That bank keeps 10% as reserves and loans out the remaining amount.


This cycle continues throughout the banking system, creating a multiple expansion of deposits and the money supply.

Money Multiplier

The money multiplier estimates the maximum amount of money that can be created from an initial deposit.

Money Multiplier Formula:

Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ Reserve Requirement


For example, if the reserve requirement is 10%, the money multiplier equals 10. An initial deposit of $1,000 could theoretically support up to $10,000 in total deposits throughout the banking system.


The actual multiplier may be smaller if banks hold excess reserves or borrowers do not redeposit funds.

Central Banking System

Central banks oversee the banking system and help maintain economic stability.

Monetary Policy

Central banks influence money supply and interest rates to achieve economic goals.

Bank Supervision

They monitor financial institutions and help ensure stability.

Lender of Last Resort

Central banks can provide emergency loans during financial crises.

Federal Reserve Structure

The Federal Reserve serves as the central banking system of the United States.

Board of Governors

Provides leadership and policy direction for the Federal Reserve System.

Regional Reserve Banks

Twelve regional banks carry out Federal Reserve functions throughout the country.

Federal Open Market Committee

The FOMC makes key decisions regarding monetary policy and interest rates.

Although AP Economics focuses on U.S. institutions, the concepts of central banking apply broadly to economies around the world.

Real-World Banking Examples

Banking systems play a vital role in economic development and financial stability.

Consumer Loans

Banks provide mortgages, auto loans, and personal loans that support household spending.

Business Financing

Companies rely on bank loans to expand operations and invest in new projects.

Financial Crises

Central banks often intervene during periods of instability to maintain confidence in financial markets.

AP Economics Connections

Money and banking concepts connect directly to many major AP Macroeconomics topics.

Monetary Policy

Understanding money creation is essential for analyzing central bank actions.

Interest Rates

Money supply changes influence borrowing costs throughout the economy.

Inflation

Rapid growth in the money supply can contribute to inflationary pressures.

Economic Growth

Bank lending supports investment and long-term economic expansion.

Financial Stability

Healthy banking systems help reduce economic volatility.

Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve's decisions influence money supply, employment, and inflation.

Summary

Money and banking are essential components of modern economies, facilitating transactions, investment, and economic growth.

This chapter explored the functions and characteristics of money, different measures of the money supply, and the role of banks in creating money through fractional reserve banking.


You also learned how reserves influence lending, how the money multiplier works, and how central banks oversee financial systems. These concepts provide the foundation for understanding monetary policy in the next chapter.

Key Terms

Important vocabulary from Chapter 16.

Money

Anything generally accepted as payment for goods and services.

Medium of Exchange

A function of money that facilitates transactions.

Store of Value

The ability of money to retain purchasing power over time.

M1

The narrowest measure of the money supply.

M2

A broader measure of the money supply that includes savings deposits.

Fractional Reserve Banking

A system where banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves.

Required Reserves

Minimum reserves banks must maintain.

Excess Reserves

Reserves held beyond the required amount.

Money Multiplier

The maximum expansion of deposits from an initial deposit.

Central Bank

The institution responsible for overseeing a nation's monetary system.

Chapter Summary

Key takeaways from Chapter 16.

  • Money serves as a medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value, and standard of deferred payment.
  • Effective money must be durable, portable, divisible, uniform, limited in supply, and widely accepted.
  • M1 and M2 are common measures of the money supply.
  • Fractional reserve banking allows banks to create money through lending.
  • Required reserves limit how much banks can lend.
  • Excess reserves represent funds available for additional lending.
  • The money multiplier measures the potential expansion of deposits.
  • Central banks oversee the financial system and monetary policy.
  • The Federal Reserve serves as the central bank of the United States.
  • Money and banking concepts form the foundation of monetary policy analysis.

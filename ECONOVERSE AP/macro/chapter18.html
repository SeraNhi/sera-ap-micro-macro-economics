Chapter 18: Economic Growth and Productivity

Explore the factors that drive long-run economic growth, improve living standards, and increase productivity. Learn how nations build wealth through investment, innovation, and efficient use of resources.

Chapter Overview

Economic growth is the foundation of rising living standards and long-term prosperity. Economists study growth to understand why some countries become wealthier over time and how governments can encourage sustainable development.

In this chapter, you will learn how economic growth is measured, why productivity matters, and how improvements in technology, education, and capital formation contribute to long-run increases in output and income.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

Define Economic Growth

Explain how economists measure long-run increases in production and income.

Analyze Productivity

Understand why productivity is a key driver of economic prosperity.

Evaluate Growth Factors

Identify the major factors that contribute to long-run economic growth.

Economic Growth

Economic growth refers to an increase in a nation's capacity to produce goods and services over time.

Economic growth allows societies to enjoy higher incomes, improved living standards, and greater opportunities. Sustained growth is one of the primary goals of macroeconomic policy.


When an economy grows, businesses produce more output, workers earn higher incomes, and governments collect more tax revenue to fund public services.

AP Economics Focus: Long-run economic growth shifts the Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) curve to the right, increasing an economy's productive capacity.

Measuring Economic Growth

Economists typically measure economic growth using changes in Real Gross Domestic Product (Real GDP).

Real GDP Growth

Measures changes in output while removing the effects of inflation.

GDP Per Capita

Measures average economic output per person and is often used to compare living standards.

Long-Term Trends

Economic growth is most meaningful when evaluated over long periods rather than short-term fluctuations.

Because population growth can affect total GDP, economists frequently examine real GDP per capita to better understand improvements in individual well-being.

Productivity

Productivity measures the amount of output produced per unit of input.

Labor Productivity

Measures output produced per worker or per hour worked.

Efficiency

Higher productivity allows more goods and services to be produced using the same resources.

Living Standards

Productivity growth is one of the most important determinants of long-run improvements in living standards.

When workers become more productive through better education, training, technology, or equipment, the economy can produce more output without requiring additional resources.


As productivity rises, businesses can often pay higher wages while remaining competitive, contributing to long-term economic growth and rising incomes.

Human Capital

Human capital refers to the knowledge, skills, education, training, and health of a workforce.

Education

More educated workers generally possess greater productivity and adaptability.

Training

Job-specific training helps workers perform tasks more efficiently and effectively.

Health

Healthy workers are often more productive and contribute more consistently to economic output.

Investments in human capital improve worker productivity and are among the most important drivers of long-run economic growth.

Physical Capital

Physical capital consists of the tools, machinery, equipment, factories, and infrastructure used to produce goods and services.

Machinery

Modern equipment allows workers to produce more output in less time.

Infrastructure

Roads, ports, airports, and communication networks support economic activity.

Capital Investment

Businesses increase productive capacity by investing in new capital goods.

As the stock of physical capital grows, workers become more productive and the economy's potential output increases.

Technological Progress

Technological progress refers to improvements in knowledge, innovation, and production methods.

Advances in technology allow businesses to produce more goods and services using the same amount of labor and capital.


Examples include automation, artificial intelligence, advanced manufacturing systems, medical innovations, and improvements in communication technology.


Many economists consider technological progress the most important long-run source of economic growth.

Investment in Education

Education plays a critical role in developing human capital and increasing productivity.

Primary Education

Provides foundational literacy, numeracy, and critical thinking skills.

Higher Education

Develops specialized knowledge needed in advanced industries.

Workforce Development

Continuous learning helps workers adapt to changing technologies and labor market demands.

Countries that invest heavily in education often experience higher productivity growth, stronger innovation, and improved living standards.

Research and Development

Research and Development (R&D) involves creating new products, technologies, and production methods.

Innovation

Research leads to new discoveries that improve productivity and efficiency.

Competitive Advantage

Businesses that innovate can gain market advantages and expand output.

Long-Term Growth

Technological breakthroughs often generate sustained economic expansion.

Governments, universities, and private firms all contribute to research and development activities that support economic growth.

Economic Growth Policies

Governments can adopt policies designed to promote long-run economic growth.

Education Investment

Improves workforce quality and productivity.

Infrastructure Spending

Enhances transportation, communication, and business efficiency.

Support for Innovation

Encourages research, entrepreneurship, and technological progress.

Stable Institutions

Strong legal systems and property rights encourage investment.

Capital Formation

Policies that encourage saving and investment increase productive capacity.

Open Markets

Trade and competition can improve efficiency and economic performance.

Real-World Growth Examples

Many countries have achieved rapid economic growth through productivity improvements and investment.

Technology Industries

Innovation in computing and telecommunications has significantly increased productivity worldwide.

Infrastructure Expansion

Major transportation and energy projects have helped support economic development.

Education Improvements

Countries investing heavily in education often experience stronger long-run growth.

Economic growth experiences vary across nations, but successful economies typically combine investment, innovation, education, and stable institutions.

Real GDP

Human Capital

Investment

Technology

Living Standards

Summary

Economic growth and productivity are the foundations of rising living standards and long-term prosperity.

This chapter examined how economies increase their productive capacity over time through investments in human capital, physical capital, technology, and innovation.


You also explored the role of education, research and development, and government policies in promoting long-run economic growth. These concepts help explain why some nations experience sustained increases in income and living standards.

Chapter Summary

Key takeaways from Chapter 18.

  • Economic growth increases a nation's productive capacity and living standards.
  • Real GDP and GDP per capita are common measures of economic growth.
  • Productivity is a major determinant of long-run prosperity.
  • Human capital includes education, skills, training, and health.
  • Physical capital consists of equipment, machinery, and infrastructure.
  • Technological progress improves efficiency and productivity.
  • Investment in education strengthens human capital.
  • Research and development drive innovation and economic advancement.
  • Government policies can support long-run growth through investment and stable institutions.
  • Economic growth shifts Long-Run Aggregate Supply to the right.

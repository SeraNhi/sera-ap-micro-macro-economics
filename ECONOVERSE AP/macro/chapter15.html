Chapter 15: Fiscal Policy

Discover how governments influence economic activity through taxation, spending, budget policies, and economic stabilization tools. Fiscal policy is one of the most important topics in AP Macroeconomics and plays a central role in managing economic growth, inflation, and unemployment.

Chapter Overview

Fiscal policy refers to the use of government spending and taxation to influence economic conditions. Governments can stimulate economic growth during recessions or slow inflation during periods of excessive expansion through carefully designed fiscal policies.

In this chapter, you will learn how fiscal policy affects Aggregate Demand, employment, inflation, economic growth, and business cycles. You will also examine how policymakers use spending programs and tax changes to achieve macroeconomic goals.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

Understand Fiscal Policy

Define fiscal policy and explain its role in stabilizing the economy.

Analyze Government Actions

Evaluate how changes in spending and taxation affect Aggregate Demand.

Interpret Economic Outcomes

Explain the effects of fiscal policy on inflation, unemployment, and GDP.

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence economic activity. Governments use fiscal policy to promote stable economic growth, reduce unemployment, and control inflation.

Fiscal policy primarily affects Aggregate Demand. By increasing spending or reducing taxes, governments can stimulate economic activity. By reducing spending or increasing taxes, governments can slow excessive growth and inflation.


Fiscal policy decisions are typically made by legislative and executive branches of government rather than central banks.

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Expansionary fiscal policy is used when an economy is experiencing slow growth or recession.

Increase Government Spending

Infrastructure projects, education investments, and public programs can increase Aggregate Demand.

Reduce Taxes

Lower taxes increase disposable income and encourage consumer spending.

Reduce Unemployment

Higher spending often leads to increased production and job creation.

Expansionary fiscal policy shifts Aggregate Demand to the right and is commonly used during economic downturns to stimulate growth.

Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Contractionary fiscal policy is used when the economy is growing too rapidly and inflation becomes a concern.

Decrease Government Spending

Reduced public spending lowers Aggregate Demand.

Increase Taxes

Higher taxes reduce consumer spending and business investment.

Control Inflation

Slowing demand can help stabilize rising prices.

Contractionary fiscal policy shifts Aggregate Demand to the left and is often used during periods of excessive inflationary pressure.

Government Spending Multiplier

The government spending multiplier measures how an initial change in government spending creates a larger overall change in GDP.

When the government spends money, that spending becomes income for households and businesses. Those recipients then spend a portion of their new income, creating additional rounds of spending throughout the economy.


Multiplier Formula:

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC)


Where MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume) represents the fraction of additional income that consumers spend rather than save.

Tax Multiplier

Changes in taxes also influence economic activity through the multiplier effect.

A tax cut increases disposable income, encouraging households to spend more. However, because some income is saved, the tax multiplier is generally smaller than the government spending multiplier.


Tax Multiplier Formula:

− MPC ÷ (1 − MPC)


The negative sign indicates that tax increases reduce GDP while tax cuts increase GDP.

Balanced Budget Multiplier

A balanced budget multiplier occurs when government spending and taxes increase by the same amount.

Even though taxes and spending rise equally, the economy still experiences an increase in Aggregate Demand because government spending has a larger direct impact than the reduction in household spending caused by higher taxes.


For AP Economics, the balanced budget multiplier is generally equal to 1.

Budget Deficits

A budget deficit occurs when government spending exceeds government revenue during a fiscal year.

Causes

Recessions, tax cuts, emergency spending, and economic stimulus programs may increase deficits.

Purpose

Deficits are often used to stimulate economic activity during periods of weak growth.

Concerns

Persistent deficits can contribute to rising government debt over time.

National Debt

The national debt represents the accumulation of past government deficits minus any surpluses.

When governments borrow money to finance deficits, they increase the national debt. Governments typically borrow by issuing bonds purchased by investors, financial institutions, and foreign governments.


Economists debate the long-term effects of large national debts. While borrowing can support economic growth during recessions, excessive debt may create future fiscal challenges.

Crowding Out Effect

The crowding out effect occurs when government borrowing competes with private borrowing for available financial resources.

As government borrowing increases, interest rates may rise. Higher interest rates can discourage private investment spending by businesses.


As a result, some of the positive effects of expansionary fiscal policy may be reduced because private-sector investment declines.


Crowding out is particularly important when the economy is operating near full employment.

Automatic Stabilizers

Automatic stabilizers are government programs that automatically help stabilize the economy without requiring new legislation.

Unemployment Benefits

Provide income support during economic downturns, helping maintain consumer spending.

Progressive Taxes

Tax revenues rise during expansions and fall during recessions, helping moderate economic fluctuations.

Transfer Payments

Government assistance programs automatically increase support when economic conditions worsen.

Real-World Fiscal Policy Examples

Fiscal policy has played an important role in addressing economic challenges around the world.

Economic Recessions

Governments often increase spending and reduce taxes to stimulate Aggregate Demand and reduce unemployment.

Financial Crises

Large-scale fiscal stimulus packages are sometimes used to stabilize financial systems and restore confidence.

Infrastructure Investment

Public projects can create jobs while improving long-term economic productivity.

Recent global economic downturns have demonstrated how governments use fiscal policy to support households, businesses, and financial institutions during periods of economic stress.

Summary

Fiscal policy is a powerful tool used by governments to influence economic activity, stabilize business cycles, and promote long-term economic growth.

Governments can use expansionary fiscal policy during recessions to stimulate Aggregate Demand and reduce unemployment. During periods of excessive inflation, contractionary fiscal policy may be used to slow economic growth and stabilize prices.


Fiscal policy also includes important concepts such as multipliers, budget deficits, national debt, crowding out, and automatic stabilizers. These concepts help economists understand both the short-run and long-run effects of government actions.

Chapter 14: Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

Understand how economy-wide spending and production determine output, employment, and price levels. Learn how economists use the Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply model to analyze inflation, recessions, economic growth, and government policy.

Chapter Overview

The Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply (AD-AS) model is one of the most important frameworks in AP Macroeconomics. It combines spending, production, inflation, unemployment, and economic growth into a single analytical model.

In this chapter, you will learn how Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply interact to determine equilibrium output and price levels. You will also explore how shifts in these curves create inflationary and recessionary gaps and how policymakers respond to economic fluctuations.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Define Aggregate Demand (AD).
  • Identify the components of Aggregate Demand.
  • Explain factors that shift Aggregate Demand.
  • Differentiate between Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply.
  • Interpret AD-AS graphs and economic outcomes.
  • Analyze recessionary and inflationary gaps.
  • Apply AD-AS concepts to real-world economic events.

Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate Demand represents the total quantity of final goods and services demanded in an economy at various price levels during a given period.

Unlike individual demand curves, Aggregate Demand measures spending across the entire economy. It reflects the combined purchasing decisions of households, businesses, governments, and foreign buyers.

Aggregate Demand Formula

AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

As the overall price level decreases, the quantity of goods and services demanded generally increases. As the price level rises, aggregate demand tends to decrease.


This inverse relationship produces the downward-sloping Aggregate Demand curve used throughout AP Macroeconomics.

Components of Aggregate Demand

Aggregate Demand consists of four major categories of spending.

Consumption (C)

Household spending on goods and services. Consumption is typically the largest component of Aggregate Demand.

Investment (I)

Business spending on capital equipment, factories, inventories, and residential construction.

Government Spending (G)

Expenditures by federal, state, and local governments on public goods and services.

Net Exports (X − M)

Exports minus imports. Positive net exports increase Aggregate Demand, while negative net exports reduce it.

Shifts in Aggregate Demand

Changes in spending behavior cause the Aggregate Demand curve to shift.

A rightward shift in Aggregate Demand indicates increased total spending in the economy. A leftward shift indicates decreased spending.


Major factors that shift Aggregate Demand include:

  • Changes in consumer confidence
  • Changes in household wealth
  • Interest rate changes
  • Business investment expectations
  • Government spending policies
  • Tax changes
  • Exchange rate fluctuations
  • Foreign economic growth

For example, if consumers become optimistic about future income, they may increase spending. This increase in consumption shifts Aggregate Demand to the right.


Similarly, expansionary fiscal or monetary policy often increases Aggregate Demand and stimulates economic activity.

Aggregate Supply (AS)

Aggregate Supply (AS) represents the total quantity of goods and services that firms are willing and able to produce at different overall price levels within an economy.

Aggregate Supply reflects the productive capacity of the economy and is influenced by resource availability, production costs, technology, and government policies.


Unlike individual supply curves studied in microeconomics, Aggregate Supply examines production across the entire economy.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

The SRAS curve shows the relationship between the overall price level and the quantity of real GDP supplied in the short run.

Upward Sloping

As prices rise, firms often increase production because profits tend to increase faster than costs in the short run.

Sticky Wages

Workers' wages and some input prices adjust slowly, causing temporary increases in profitability.

Short-Run Production

Businesses can expand output by utilizing existing resources more intensively.

Factors that shift SRAS include changes in wages, resource costs, taxes, subsidies, and productivity.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

The LRAS curve represents the economy's maximum sustainable level of production when all resources are fully employed.

In the long run, output is determined by the quantity and quality of resources, technology, and productivity rather than the price level.


Because long-run production does not depend on the price level, the LRAS curve is typically shown as a vertical line.


Economic growth shifts LRAS to the right, allowing the economy to produce more goods and services over time.

Equilibrium Output and Price Level

Macroeconomic equilibrium occurs where Aggregate Demand intersects Aggregate Supply.

Equilibrium Output

The quantity of real GDP produced and purchased in the economy.

Equilibrium Price Level

The overall average level of prices in the economy.

Economic Stability

When AD and AS are balanced, the economy operates efficiently.

Recessionary Gap

A recessionary gap occurs when equilibrium real GDP falls below the economy's full-employment level.

Recessionary gaps are associated with unemployment, underutilized resources, and slower economic growth.


Governments and central banks often use expansionary fiscal or monetary policies to reduce recessionary gaps.

Inflationary Gap

An inflationary gap exists when equilibrium output exceeds the economy's sustainable full-employment level.

Although production may temporarily rise above potential output, resource shortages and labor market pressures often create inflation.


Policymakers may implement contractionary policies to slow economic activity and reduce inflationary pressures.

Economic Fluctuations

Economies naturally experience periods of expansion and contraction known as business cycles.

Expansion

Increasing output, rising employment, and economic growth.

Peak

The highest point of economic activity before growth begins to slow.

Recession

A period of declining economic activity and rising unemployment.

Recovery

Economic improvement following a recession.

Real-World AD-AS Applications

The AD-AS model helps economists understand major economic events and policy decisions.

COVID-19 Pandemic

Global supply disruptions shifted SRAS left while reduced spending affected Aggregate Demand.

Government Stimulus

Fiscal stimulus programs increase Aggregate Demand by boosting spending.

Technological Innovation

Advances in technology increase productivity and shift LRAS outward.

AP Economics Connections

Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply form one of the most important models in AP Macroeconomics.

Students should be able to draw AD-AS graphs, identify shifts, analyze recessionary and inflationary gaps, and explain the effects of fiscal and monetary policies on output and price levels.


AD-AS analysis frequently appears in both Multiple-Choice Questions (MCQs) and Free-Response Questions (FRQs) on the AP Macroeconomics exam.

Summary

Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply form the foundation of modern macroeconomic analysis. Understanding how spending, production, and price levels interact helps economists explain inflation, unemployment, economic growth, and business cycles.

Aggregate Demand represents total spending within an economy, while Aggregate Supply represents total production. Their interaction determines equilibrium output and the overall price level.


Changes in consumer spending, investment, government purchases, and net exports can shift Aggregate Demand. Changes in production costs, resource availability, and productivity can shift Aggregate Supply.


Understanding recessionary and inflationary gaps allows policymakers to evaluate economic conditions and design appropriate fiscal and monetary policies.

Key Terms

Important vocabulary from Chapter 14.

Aggregate Supply (AS)

LRAS

Equilibrium Output

Fluctuations in economic activity over time.

Chapter Summary

Essential ideas from Chapter 14.

  • Aggregate Demand measures total spending within an economy.
  • Consumption, Investment, Government Spending, and Net Exports determine AD.
  • Aggregate Supply measures total production.
  • SRAS and LRAS describe production in different time horizons.
  • Equilibrium occurs where AD and AS intersect.
  • Recessionary gaps indicate economic weakness.
  • Inflationary gaps indicate excessive economic expansion.
  • Business cycles reflect fluctuations in economic activity.
  • Policymakers use AD-AS analysis to evaluate economic conditions.

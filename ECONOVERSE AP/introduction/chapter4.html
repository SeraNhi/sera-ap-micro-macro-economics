Chapter 4

Demand and Supply: The Basics

Discover how markets determine prices and quantities through the interaction of buyers and sellers. This chapter introduces one of the most important models in all of economics.

Chapter Overview

Demand and supply form the foundation of modern economic analysis. These concepts explain how markets allocate resources, determine prices, and respond to changes in consumer behavior, production costs, and economic conditions.

Almost every market outcome can be understood using demand and supply analysis. Whether studying housing prices, airline tickets, smartphones, gasoline, or labor markets, economists begin by examining how buyers and sellers interact.


In this chapter, you will learn the Law of Demand, understand the factors that influence consumer purchasing decisions, and prepare for the supply analysis that follows later in the chapter.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Define demand and explain its importance.
  • Understand the Law of Demand.
  • Distinguish between a change in demand and a change in quantity demanded.
  • Identify factors that shift demand.
  • Interpret demand curves and demand schedules.
  • Analyze consumer behavior using economic reasoning.
  • Apply demand concepts to real-world market situations.
  • Prepare for supply and market equilibrium analysis.

Introduction

Markets coordinate millions of decisions every day.

Why does the price of concert tickets increase when demand is high? Why do retailers offer discounts during slow seasons? Why do luxury brands often maintain high prices?


Economists answer these questions using demand and supply analysis. The interaction of buyers and sellers creates market outcomes that determine prices and quantities exchanged.


Demand represents the behavior of consumers, while supply represents the behavior of producers. Together, these forces form the foundation of market economies.

Demand and the Law of Demand

Demand reflects consumers' willingness and ability to purchase goods and services.

Demand refers to the quantities of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a specific period of time.


The Law of Demand states that, all else equal, as the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases. Conversely, when the price of a good decreases, the quantity demanded increases.


This inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded is one of the most fundamental principles in economics.


Consumers generally respond to lower prices by purchasing more because:

  • Goods become more affordable.
  • Consumers can substitute away from more expensive alternatives.
  • Purchasing power effectively increases.
  • Additional units provide value at lower costs.

As a result, demand curves typically slope downward from left to right, illustrating the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.

Demand Schedule

A demand schedule is a table that shows the quantity of a good consumers are willing and able to purchase at different prices.

Economists often begin demand analysis using a demand schedule. The schedule demonstrates the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.


Price ($) Quantity Demanded
10 100
8 140
6 180
4 240
2 320

Notice that as price decreases, quantity demanded increases. This pattern reflects the Law of Demand.

Demand Curve

A demand curve graphically represents the relationship between price and quantity demanded.

The demand curve slopes downward from left to right because consumers generally buy more of a product when its price falls.


Economists use demand curves to predict consumer behavior, analyze market outcomes, and evaluate policy impacts.


A movement along the demand curve reflects a change in price, while a shift of the entire curve indicates a change in demand.


On the AP Exam, students are frequently asked to draw, label, interpret, and explain demand curves.

Changes in Quantity Demanded

A change in quantity demanded occurs when the price of the good changes.

When the price changes, consumers move from one point to another on the same demand curve.


Increase in Quantity Demanded

Occurs when price falls and consumers purchase more of the good.


Decrease in Quantity Demanded

Occurs when price rises and consumers purchase less of the good.


Remember: Price changes cause movements along the demand curve.

Changes in Demand

A change in demand occurs when factors other than price change.

When demand changes, the entire demand curve shifts either to the right or to the left.


Increase in Demand

Demand shifts right. Consumers are willing to purchase more at every price level.


Decrease in Demand

Demand shifts left. Consumers are willing to purchase less at every price level.


One of the most common AP Economics mistakes is confusing movements along the curve with shifts of the curve.

Determinants of Demand

Several factors can cause demand to increase or decrease.

Consumer Income

Higher incomes generally increase demand for normal goods.

Consumer Preferences

Changes in tastes and trends affect purchasing decisions.

Population

More consumers often increase market demand.

Expectations

Future expectations can influence current buying behavior.

Substitute Goods

An increase in the price of one substitute may increase demand for another.

Complementary Goods

Demand for related products often moves together.

Real-World Demand Examples

Demand analysis helps explain many everyday economic situations.

Smartphones

New smartphone releases often increase consumer demand due to improved technology and marketing campaigns.


Airline Tickets

Demand frequently increases during holidays and vacation periods, causing ticket prices to rise.


Coffee Shops

Consumer preferences and changing lifestyles can increase demand for specialty coffee products.


Streaming Services

New content and improved features often shift consumer demand toward specific platforms.

Supply and the Law of Supply

Supply represents the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at different price levels during a specific period of time.

Understanding Supply

The Law of Supply states that, all else being equal, producers are willing to supply more of a good as its price increases. Higher prices often create greater opportunities for profit, encouraging firms to expand production.


Conversely, when prices fall, producers may reduce output because production becomes less profitable.


Determinants of Supply

  • Input Costs
  • Technology
  • Taxes and Subsidies
  • Number of Sellers
  • Producer Expectations
  • Natural Events

A change in any determinant other than price causes the entire supply curve to shift.

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs when quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

At equilibrium, buyers and sellers agree on both price and quantity. There is no tendency for the market price to change because there is neither excess demand nor excess supply.


Economists use equilibrium analysis to understand how markets respond to changing conditions and why prices naturally move toward balance over time.


Equilibrium Price

The market-clearing price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

Equilibrium Quantity

The amount bought and sold at the equilibrium price.

Surplus and Shortage

Disequilibrium occurs whenever the market price differs from the equilibrium price.

Surplus

A surplus occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Producers are unable to sell all units offered for sale.


Surpluses place downward pressure on prices, encouraging producers to reduce output and consumers to purchase more.

Shortage

A shortage occurs when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied.


Shortages place upward pressure on prices, encouraging producers to increase output while reducing consumer demand.

    B. Increase

    D. Become negative

  2. Which factor would cause demand to increase?

    A. Lower consumer income for a normal good
    B. Negative consumer expectations
    C. Increase in consumer income
    D. Increase in production costs

  3. A market shortage occurs when:

    A. Quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded
    B. Quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied
    C. Supply equals demand
    D. Prices remain constant
